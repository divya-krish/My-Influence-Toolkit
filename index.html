üí™ Build Influence without Authority

Legitimacy, not title, creates lasting influence.

Course Concept: Formal authority alone does not create influence-legitimacy does. As discussed in class and demonstrated by Sergio Vieira de Mello, authority without legitimacy has limited impact. Legitimacy must be earned through competence, participation, and demonstrating value to stakeholders. Similarly, Janette Sadik-Khan influenced major citywide changes not through authority alone, but by building credibility through logos, pilots, and demonstrated results. Influence comes from informal power sources such as expertise, relationships, and credibility - not positional hierarchy.

My Insight: In my role, I often need to influence decisions across teams and senior leadership where I do not have formal authority. I cannot rely on my title or hierarchy to drive outcomes. Instead, my influence depends on how others perceive my competence, judgment, and credibility. I've realized that legitimacy is not granted automatically and that it is earned through consistent performance, preparation, and how I show up in relationships. The quote that stuck with me from class is 'Leaders are always on stage'.

Action: I plan to be intentional and focus on building credibility through preparation, thoughtful contributions, and consistently delivering results. I will avoid relying on positional arguments and instead demonstrate value through competence and insight. Over time, I plan to build influence by repeatedly removing barriers for others just like de Mello did and becoming someone others trust and seek out.

üß≠ Match Influence Strategy to Situation

Influence succeeds when tactics match context.

Course Concept: There is no single best influence strategy. As discussed in class, effective influence depends on situational awareness - understanding stakeholders, incentives, constraints, and context. Janette Sadik-Khan successfully implemented controversial initiatives by adapting her approach to different stakeholders, using data, pilots, and framing to reduce resistance and build legitimacy. Influence requires diagnosing the situation before acting. AOC's quote 'If you are too expedient in your decision or too dismissive, you'll be unrecognizable' resonated strongly with me.

My Insight: Influence is not about applying the same tactics everywhere, but understanding situational awareness. Situational awareness of how the game is played and the intentionality of whether I want to do it or not. I've realized that misreading context weakens my influence, while situational awareness increases effectiveness.

Action: Before attempting to influence decisions, I will assess stakeholder motivations, incentives, and concerns. I will adapt my communication and influence approach based on the situation rather than relying on my default patterns. I plan to reduce non-promotable tasks that do not help me expand my influence.

üí¨ Influence through Persuasion, not Force

Persuasion creates commitment; force creates resistance.

Course Concept: As discussed in class, persuasion creates internalized commitment, while coercion creates resistance. This is reflected in both the JSK and AOC cases, where influence was achieved by shaping how stakeholders interpreted issues, rather than forcing compliance. Persuasion relies on credibility, logic, and emotional resonance. Sustainable influence changes how people think - not just how they act. Simonsohn and Simmons' research on persuasion offered some valuable insights on how people behave.

My Insight: When people genuinely believe in an idea, they commit to it more fully. Influence built on persuasion strengthens relationships and long-term credibility. Force may achieve short-term compliance, but persuasion builds lasting influence.

Action: I plan to focus on framing my ideas in ways that align with stakeholders' priorities and motivations. I shall use logic, credibility, and clarity to help others understand and support my recommendations. At the same time, I will be mindful of the intentions of others and not assume that I know them. Also the quote mentioned in class 'If it feels too good to say it, Don't!' was a good reminder that words matter.

üìä When presenting to senior leadership

Context: Quarterly business reviews and strategic discussions where I need executive buy-in for initiatives that I do not have authority to mandate.

My Specific Tactic: I plan to lead with logos - data, logic, and credibility, and frame my recommendation as the logical solution to a problem leadership already cares about. I will begin by clearly defining the business risk or opportunity, using metrics and external benchmarks to establish urgency and legitimacy. At the same time, I will be mindful of Heath and Heath's warning that knowing a lot can lead to sharing too much - I shall keep my message clear and focused. I will use the 'meeting before the meeting' approach to understand stakeholder concerns and build early support. Then, I will position my recommendation not as my idea, but as the inevitable next step based on evidence. Throughout, I will remain calm and intentional, avoiding defensive reactions to questions. I will use JSK's approach and build influence by establishing legitimacy first, then guiding decision-makers toward the conclusion.

Influence Principles to Apply:

  • Legitimacy through expertise and preparation: As discussed in class and reinforced in the JSK Times Square case, leaders must earn legitimacy through competence and data, especially when proposing bold changes without formal authority. Janette Sadik-Khan convinced Mayor Bloomberg not through positional power, but by presenting compelling analysis and framing the proposal as a solution to a shared problem.
  • Framing to align with executive priorities: JSK's team reframed the pedestrian plaza not as a public space initiative but as a congestion and economic solution, making it easier for stakeholders to support it. As discussed in class, influence succeeds when ideas are framed in terms of the audience's goals.
  • Authority and social proof to reduce perceived risk: As discussed in class and reinforced in multiple cases, citing external benchmarks, competitor actions, and objective data signals that the recommendation is credible and aligned with broader trends.

  • Understanding the leader's incentives and constraints: As discussed in class, influence requires seeing the situation from the other person's perspective. Senior leaders prioritize risk mitigation, organizational alignment, and strategic outcomes.
  • Consistency and commitment to reinforce alignment: Once leaders publicly support an idea, they are more likely to continue supporting it, reinforcing commitment over time.

Influence Principles to Apply:

  • Earn legitimacy through competence and contribution: As discussed in class and reinforced by AOC's example, individuals can rapidly build influence by demonstrating expertise, preparation, and thoughtful engagement.
  • Observation before action to understand power dynamics: As discussed in class, effective influencers diagnose the situation before acting, understanding stakeholders, incentives, and informal power structures.

üõ°Ô∏è When influencing skeptical or resistant stakeholders

Context: Situations where stakeholders are resistant to change due to risk aversion, competing priorities, or lack of trust.

My Specific Tactic: I will acknowledge concerns, validate stakeholder perspectives, and use data and small experiments to demonstrate value. Influence Principles to Apply:

  • Legitimacy through evidence and experimentation: JSK used pilot programs and measurable data to reduce perceived risk and build credibility.
  • Persuasion over force: As discussed in class, sustainable influence comes from changing how people see a situation, not forcing compliance.
